Spartan Race Ultra - Killington Vermont

30.5 miles, 14k elevation, 13:05 hours. 34% completion rate

Training

Prior to this race, I lived in Herald Towers which is a 24 floor luxury building in midtown Manhattan. My training included: stair climbers in the fire exit (24-story),four to six times; ruck PT using my weighted rucksack; running. I ran with Orchard Street Runners and Old Man Run Club. I trained four to five times a week.

For two nights, I stayed in my rental car (Toyota Camry 2018) at a campsite. I and five other racers did the same. Mcdonalds was our safe haven. If you ever end homeless, I recommend staying near a Mcdonalds because it offers: free wifi, bathrooms, and convenient hours. Starbucks and other competing fast food locations more or less offer the same, but Mcdonalds has extended hours. The Mcdonalds, which was adjacent to the campsite, was open 5am - 12am which was almost 24/7.

The race began at 7am. I went hard and I led the last Ultra wave heat. I passed Open and Age Group racers who started in the 06:45am, 06:30am, and 06:15am heat. Only a small percentage of people will finish, so I had to move viligant and time efficient. This course features extended up hill segments & steep downhills. Flat sections are a rarity. I ran through every flat segment, and even some of the downhills. Time was an enemy.

Reflecting on my race. I never stopped moving. For 13 hours straight, I was more or less, in constant motion. When I reached the water obstacles, my body was not even phased by the cold water. I dived in head first, the cold lake water helped recalibrate my muscles and erased my fatigue.

If you have done a spartan race before, you know that this event requires a well rounded physique. Depending on the obstacle, your grip, upper body strength, legs, or core will be put to the test. I have not raced since 2019. Each obstacle felt like a friend I haven't seen in a long time.

Mile 3 featured the notorious Killington Death March. This segment was long & difficult, but not as difficult compared to the ultra loop. The ultra loop (Death March 2) was not as hard compared to the incline at mile 12 to mile 13. I’m going to reference mile 12 - mile 13 the Angry March because this segment where you saw racers position themselves on the sideline, breathing heavily, and staring into the distant void. This segment was difficult. The incline was brutal. During my traversal I had to toggle between bear crawling and hiking in order to move forward. Admittedly. moral was pretty low. This incline was tough, so I started tapping internally. In a situation like this, I think sadness and depression was useless. Instead I used anger and ferocity. I looked internal and was fueled by anger and rage. I would like to think I have a calm and chill demeanor for this section, I let the beast out. Anger and rage helped push me through this evolution.

I hit transition at 1:45pm. The cut off for transition is 2:00pm. You have to exit the transition area by 2:00 otherwise you get get disqualified. When I hit transition I immediately grabbed my Gatorade, protein bars & gels, and drank about ¾ of my gallon of water. I probably spent about two minutes in the transition area, and then started my second lap.

During the second lap, I noticed some Ultra & Beast racers inadvertently avoided running & walking through the mud. They preferred to take "clean" route with the least amount of water & mud. Instead of taking sharp turns, and cutting corners, they would take this longer path. Unlike them, I didn’t give a fuck. I narrowed the gap, and took the most optimal approach even if I had to step through a foot of mud. Mud, dirt, sand, and water did not even phase me. I even coated my face and body in it because my muscles were fatigued and the cool feeling can delay the lactic acid build up. During these congested segments in the woods, my strategy allowed me to pass a lot of racers. I needed to do this in order to make the next time cut off. .

Around mile 19 - 21 I hit the “ wall”. My face went numb, my chest was vibrating, and my heartrate was high. I have zero medical experience, but I believe these are symptoms of hyponatremia, low blood sugar, and dehydration. Rarely do I succumb to the internal dialogue between my brain and body, but this time I almost conceived. The gatorade bottle, that I carried during my second lap, was my lifeline. When your body is starving for nutrition hits differently. Any fuel correlates to a direct energy boost. Your senses are elevated. (Nutrition, Nutrition, Nutrition. Bring a feast, because you are going to need it. For this race, expect to consume calories every 45 minute. I did not have a camelbak or water reservoir on me during the race, therefore I was dependent on the water stations. There were a total of 19 water stations, and I drank 16-24 oz of water at every water station.)

My mantra during the entire race was: “The only way I will stop is if they pull me off the course, otherwise I'm not stopping”. Failure is inevitable, but quitting is not. If I get ripped off the course from a race official, I can sleep peacefully knowing I gave it my all.

The Killington Ultra is not a “fun” race. This event was a true testament of my endurance. There is a reason this venue is the birthplace for Spartan race and it lives up to its name. This race was brutal, and was exactly what I was looking for. I wanted to be humbled. I wanted my mind, body, and heart to be tested. My tenacity, grit, and perseverance were pushed to a new level.
