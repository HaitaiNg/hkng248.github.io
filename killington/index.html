Spartan Race Ultra - Killington Vermont

30.5 miles, 14k elevation, 13:05 hours. 34% completion rate

Introduction This written document summarizes my solo trip to Killington, Vermont. For this vacation, I wanted to feel discomfort & misery because it helps me be thankful and appreciative for normality.

Training

Prior to this race, I lived in Herald Towers which is a 26th floor luxury building in midtown Manhattan. My training included: stair climbers in the fire exit (24-story), four to six times; ruck PT using my weighted rucksack; running with Orchard Street Runners and Old Man Run Club. I trained four to five times a week.

Solo trip. For two nights, I stayed in my rental car (Toyota Camry 2019) at a campsite. (If you ever decide to sleep in your car in Vermont in September, bring extra layers or an actual blanket. On Friday night, I felt like I almost froze to death. If it was not for my space blanket, .... just kidding. The space blanket I had was no better than a one hundred gallon trash bag). Mcdonalds was my safe haven. I now know that if I ever end up homeless, I'm going to stay near a Mcdonalds because it offers: free wifi, bathrooms, and convenient hours. Starbucks, and other competing fast food locations more or less offer the same, but Mcdonalds has extended hours. The Mcdonalds was adjacent to the campsite and was open 5am - 12am which was almost 24/7.

I went hard and I led the last Ultra wave heat 07:00am. I passed Open and Age Group racers who started at 06:45am, 06:30am, and 06:15am heat. This course featured extended up hill segments & steep downhills. Flat sections are a rarity. I ran through every flat segment, and even some of the downhills. (Remember only a small percentage of people will finish, you have to be above average, and be quick and efficient).

I never stopped moving. For 13 hours straight, I was more or less, in constant motion. When I reached the water obstacles, my body was not even shocked by the cold water. The cold lake water helped recalibrate my muscles and erased my fatigue. (This was an evolution. I used to dread cold water during events, but now I learned to endure it).

Mile three featured the famous Death March. The ultra loop (Death March II) followed afterwards. Neither of these were as difficult compared to mile twelve to mile thirteen. I’m going to reference mile twelve - mile thirteen as the "Angry March" because this sections was where a lot of racers got destroyed. (Throughout the entire race, there were always racers positioned on the sideline, breathing heavily, and staring into the distant void. During the Angry March, there were multiple "groups" of racers destroyed). The incline during the Angry March was very steep. During my ascent, I had to toggle between bear crawling and hiking in order to move forward. My moral was low, and I got pissed. Rarely, do I let my "anger and ferocity" surface but in this situation, I thought it was acceptable to do so. I let the inner beast out, and I went hard as fuck for the rest of the of the first lap. (Only during endurance events do I feel this level of intensity is acceptable).

I hit transition at 1:45pm. The cut off for transition is 2:00pm. You have to exit the transition area by 2:00 otherwise you get disqualified. At transition I immediately grabbed my Gatorade, protein bars & gels, and drank about ¾ of my gallon of water. I spent about two minutes in the transition area, and then started my second lap.

During the second lap, I was more observant. I noticed some Ultra & Beast racers inadvertently avoided running & walking through the mud. They preferred to take the "clean" route with the least amount of water & mud. Instead of taking sharp turns, and cutting corners, they would take this longer path. Unlike them, I did not give a fuck. I narrowed the gap, and took the most optimal path even if I had to step through a foot of mud. Mud, dirt, sand, and water did not even phase me. I even coated my face and body in it because my muscles were fatigued and the cool feeling helped the lactic acid build up. During these congested segments in the woods, my "I don't give a fuck" strategy allowed me to manuever around and pass a lot of racers. I needed to do this in order to make the next time cut off.

Around mile twenty I hit the “endurance wall”. The left side of my face went numb, my chest was vibrating, and my heartrate was high. I believe these are symptoms of hyponatremia, low blood sugar, and dehydration. Rarely do I succumb to the internal dialogue between my brain and body, but this time I almost conceived. The Gatorade bottle, that I carried during my second lap, was my lifeline. When your body is starving for nutrition, nutrition hits differently. Any caloric intake correlates to a direct energy boost. (Nutrition, Nutrition, Nutrition. Bring a feast, because you are going to need it. For this race, expect to consume calories every 45 minutes. Do you need a camelbak? I did not have a camelbak or water reservoir on me during the race, therefore I was dependent on the water stations. There were 19 water stations, and I drank 16-24 oz of water at every water station.)

My mantra during the entire race was: “The only way I will stop is if they pull me off the course, otherwise I'm not stopping”. Failure is inevitable, but quitting is not. If I get ripped off the course from a race official, I can sleep peacefully knowing I gave it my all.

Final thoughts. This race was brutal, and destroyed me. It was exactly what I was looking for. I wanted to be humbled. I wanted my mind, body, and heart to be tested. My tenacity, grit, and perseverance were pushed to a new level.
