Spartan Race Ultra - Killington Vermont

30.5 miles, 14k elevation, 13:05 hours. 34% completion rate

Training

Prior to this race, I lived in Herald Towers which is a 24 floor luxury building in midtown Manhattan. My training included: stair climbers in the fire exit (24-story),four to six times; ruck PT using my weighted rucksack; running. I ran with Orchard Street Runners and Old Man Run Club. I trained four to five times a week.

For two nights, I stayed in my rental car (Toyota Camry 2018) at a campsite. I and five other racers did the same. Mcdonalds was my safe haven. If you ever end up or voluntarily try being homeless,I recommend staying near a Mcdonalds because it offers: free wifi, bathrooms, and convenient hours. Starbucks and competing fast food locations more or less offer the same, but Mcdonalds hours are extended. The Mcdonalds was adjacent to the campsite and open 5am - 12am which was almost 24/7.

The race began at 7am. I went hard and I led the the last Ultra wave heat. During my traversal up the mountain, I passed Open and Age Group racers who started at 645am, 630am, and 615am heat. This race had a completion rate of 30% so I had to be vigilant and move quickly. Only a small percentage of people will finish, so I had to move quickly. (If you plan on doing this race, try and get an earlier start time). Killington is notorious for its elevation, and incline. A majority of this race featured steep uphill and downhills. I ran through every flat segment, and even some of the downhills. Time was an enemy.

I do not have a fitness watch. However I do run a lot, and I know that my pace was relatively quick. Reflecting on my race. I never stopped moving. When I reached the water obstacles, my body was not even phased by the cold water. I dived in head first, and the water helped recalibrate my muscles and erased my fatigue.

If you have done a spartan race before, you know that this event requires a well rounded physique. As you encounter obstacles your grip, upper body strength, legs, will be put to the test. I have not raced since 2019, each obstacle felt like a friend I haven't seen in a long time.

Mile 3 featured the accredited Killington Death March. This segment was easier compared to the ultra loop. The ultra loop (Death March 2) was not as hard compared to the incline at mile 12 to mile 13. I’m going to call mile 12 - mile 13 the Angry March because this segment featured the steepest incline and this was where racers got destroyed mentally and physically. The Angry March is where I saw racers sitting on the side, breathing heavily, and staring into the distant void. This segment was difficult, as I had to toggle between hiking and bear crawling in order to move forward. It was a soul crushing march. I find it useless to be sad and depressed, instead I was fueled by rage and anger. I tapped inside, and I got pissed. This side of me rarely comes out, and I used this to help and I used this to help push myself through this evolution.

I hit transition at 1:45pm. The cut off for transition is 2:00pm. When I hit transition I grabbed my Gatorade, stuffed my nutrition in my pockets, destroyed my m&ms and drank about ¾ of my gallon of water. I probably spent about 3 minutes in the transition area, and then started my second lap.

Dark thoughts: During the second lap, I noticed some Ultra & Beast racers inadvertently avoided running / walking through the mud. They preferred to take the path with the minimal amount of water even if it required wider turns, which were not as efficient.. Instead of taking sharp turns, and cutting corners, they would take this extended route. At this point, I didn’t give a fuck. I wanted to narrow the gap, and take the most optimal approach even if I had to run through 1 - 2 feet of mud. Mud, dirt, and sand did not even phase me. I even coated my body in it because my muscles were fatigued and the cool feeling can delay the lactic acid build up.

< During this race I underestimated how much I would burn during this race. I burned through my nutrition during the first lap, and started to ration and conserve my remaining fuel. I did not have a camelbak during the race, therefore I was dependent on the water stations. There were a total of 19 water stations, and I drank 16-24 oz of water at every water station. During my second lap, I carried a 32oz gatorade and refilled it at every station.

Around mile 19 - 21 I hit the “ wall”. My face went numb, my chest was vibrating, and my heartrate was high. I have zero medical experience, but I believe these are symptoms of hyponatremia, low blood sugar, and dehydration. Rarely do I succumb to the internal dialogue between my brain and body, but this time I almost conceived. The gatorade bottle was my lifeline and without it I would have DNF'd. When your body is starving for nutrition, nutrition hits differently. Any fuel correlates to a direct energy boost. (Nutrition, Nutrition, Nutrition. Bring a feast, because you are going to need it. For this race, expect to consume calories every 45 minutes. Every ascent

My mantra during the entire race was: “If I voluntarily quit it will haunt me forever, The only way I will stop is if they pull me off the course, otherwise I'm going to keep going”. For me there are two outcomes: voluntarily quit or be removed by force. Failure is inevitable, but quitting is not. If I get ripped off the course from a race official, I can sleep peacefully knowing I gave it my all.

The Killington Ultra is not a “fun” race. This event is for the serious athletes, and for those seeking something within. This course is brutal, and you are pushed to a new limit. Your mind, body and heart are going to have an internal dialogue. Prepare to be humbled. This event is a testament of your endurance. My tenacity, grit, and perseverance were pushed to a new level.

I commend and applaud anyone who participated in this event. Regardless if you DNF’d or finished, I believe we both had the opportunity to learn more about ourselves. Our paths were aligned and we were able to embrace the suck together.
