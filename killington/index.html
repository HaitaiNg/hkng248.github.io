Spartan Race Ultra - Killington Vermont

30.5 miles, 14k elevation, 13:05 hours. 34% completion rate.

Introduction

This written document summarizes my solo trip to Killington, Vermont 09.10.21 - 09.12.21. For this vacation, I wanted to feel discomfort & misery because it helps me be appreciative and thankful for normality.

Training

Prior to this race, I lived in Herald Towers which is a 26th storey luxury building in midtown Manhattan. My training included: stair climbers in the fire exit (24-story), four to six times; ruck PT using my weighted rucksack; running with Orchard Street Runners and Old Man Run Club. I trained four to five times a week.

Solo trip. For two nights, I stayed in my rental car (Toyota Camry 2019) at a campsite. (If you ever decide to sleep in your car in Vermont in September, I recommend you bring extra layers or an actual blanket. On Friday night, I felt like I almost froze to death. My space blanket was no better than a one hundred gallon trash bag. It was useless). Mcdonalds was my safe haven. I now know that if I ever end up homeless, I'm going to stay near a Mcdonalds because it offers: free wifi, bathrooms, and convenient hours. Starbucks, and other competing fast food locations more or less offer the same, but Mcdonalds has extended hours. The Mcdonalds was adjacent to the campsite and was open 5am - 12am which was almost 24/7. (If you are wondering how I showered. I didn't. I tried my best, and I used the water hose at the Spartan Race venue to clean myself)

I went hard and I led the last Ultra wave heat 07:00am. I passed Open and Age Group racers who started at 06:45am, 06:30am, 06:15am & 06:00am heat. This course featured extended up hill segments & steep downhills. Flat sections are a rarity. I ran through every flat segment, and even some of the downhills.

I never stopped moving. For 13 hours straight, I was more or less, in constant motion. When I reached the water obstacles, my body was not even shocked by the cold water. The cold lake water actually helped recalibrate my muscles and erased my fatigue. (This was an evolution. I used to dread cold water during events, but now I learned to endure it).

Mile three featured the famous Death March, mile five was the ultra loop (Death March II). Neither of these were as difficult compared to mile twelve to mile thirteen. I’m going to reference mile twelve - mile thirteen as the "Angry March" because this section was where a lot of racers got destroyed. (Throughout the entire race, there were always racers positioned on the sideline, breathing heavily, and staring into the distant void. During the Angry March, there were "groups" of racers collectively destroyed). The incline during the Angry March was very steep. During my ascent, I had to toggle between bear crawl and hiking in order to move forward. My moral was low, and I got pissed. Rarely, do I let my "anger and ferocity" surface but in this situation, I thought it was acceptable to do so. Anger and rage fueled me and helped push me through the rest of the first lap.

I hit transition at 1:45pm. The cut off for transition is 2:00pm. You have to exit the transition area by 2:00pm otherwise you get disqualified. At transition I immediately grabbed my Gatorade, protein bars & gels, and drank about ¾ of my gallon of water. I spent about two minutes in the transition area, and then started my second lap.

During the second lap, I was more observant. I noticed some Ultra & Beast racers inadvertently avoided running & walking through the mud. They preferred to take the "clean" route with the least amount of water & mud. Instead of taking sharp turns, and cutting corners, they would take this longer path. Unlike them, I did not give a fuck. I narrowed the gap, and took the most optimal path even if I had to step through a foot of mud. Mud, dirt, sand, and water did not even phase me. I even coated my face and body in it because my muscles were fatigued and the cool feeling helped the lactic acid build up. During these congested segments in the woods, my "I don't give a fuck" strategy allowed me to manuever around and pass a lot of racers. I needed to do this in order to make the next time cut off.

Around mile twenty one one I hit the “endurance wall”. The left side of my face went numb, my chest was vibrating, and my heartrate was high. I believe these are symptoms of hyponatremia, low blood sugar, and dehydration. Rarely do I succumb to the internal dialogue between my brain and body, but this time I almost conceived. The Gatorade bottle, that I carried during my second lap, was my lifeline. When your body is starving for nutrition, nutrition hits differently. Any caloric intake correlates to a direct energy boost. (Nutrition, Nutrition, Nutrition. Bring a feast, because you are going to need it. For this race, expect to consume calories every 45 minutes. Do you need a camelbak? I did not have a camelbak or water reservoir on me during the race, therefore I was dependent on the water stations. There were 19 water stations, and I drank 16-24 oz of water at every water station.)

My mantra during the entire race was: “The only way I will stop is if they pull me off the course, otherwise I'm not stopping”. Failure is inevitable, but quitting is not. If I get ripped off the course from a race official, I can sleep peacefully knowing I gave it my all. I finished at 8:05:31pm. (16/44 Overall Open Heat; 16/42 Gender; 5/5 Age 25-29).

Final thoughts. This race was brutal, and destroyed me. It was exactly what I was looking for. I wanted to be humbled. I wanted my mind, body, and heart to be tested. My tenacity, grit, and perseverance were pushed to a new level.
